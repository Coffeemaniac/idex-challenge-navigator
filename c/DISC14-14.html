Indigenous Development and Manufacturing of Microbolometer-Based Uncooled Infrared Focal Plane Arrays (FPAs)

Problem Statement

Uncooled thermal imaging systems play a critical role in surveillance, reconnaissance, target detection, industrial monitoring, and safety applications. The core sensing element in these systems is the microbolometer infrared focal plane array, which detects thermal radiation without the need for cryogenic cooling.
Despite widespread use of thermal imagers across defense and civilian domains, high-performance microbolometer FPAs are largely imported, as their fabrication involves advanced MEMS processes, specialised infrared absorber materials, precision vacuum packaging, and sophisticated read-out electronics. The absence of an integrated domestic manufacturing capability presents a strategic technological gap.

Challenge Brief / Definition

A large number of thermal imaging systems are currently deployed across surveillance, reconnaissance, perimeter security, and targeting roles. Many of these systems rely on uncooled microbolometer detectors for infrared sensing due to their compact size, low power requirements, and operational simplicity. However, existing solutions often function as standalone imaging units with limited optimisation for performance consistency, environmental robustness, and system- level integration.
At present, variations in thermal drift, noise performance, and non-uniformity across operating conditions can affect image clarity and detection reliability. Additionally, many microbolometer modules lack advanced onboard processing for real-time image correction, calibration, and health monitoring, leading to increased dependence on external processing and reduced operational efficiency.
There is a need for an integrated, high-reliability microbolometer imaging core that delivers stable thermal imaging performance across diverse environmental conditions. The system should incorporate on-chip or embedded signal conditioning, real-time non-uniformity correction (NUC), and intelligent temperature compensation to ensure consistent image quality.
Designed with a scalable and modular architecture, the solution must support seamless integration with optics, processing electronics, and display systems. It should enable low-latency video output, support AI-enabled image enhancement, and ensure compatibility with handheld, vehicle-mounted, and airborne platforms while maintaining low power consumption and high operational durability.

Existing Solution

(if Current systems rely predominantly on imported microbolometer modules. Domestic efforts are largely limited to system-level integration with minimal control over detector fabrication.

Technology Domains

Based on established infrared sensor development frameworks, the proposed microbolometer programme spans three core technology domains:-
Hardware / Sensor Domain – Design and fabrication of microbolometer focal plane arrays (FPA), MEMS absorber structures, thermal isolation legs, vacuum packaging, and pixel- level integration with ROIC (Readout Integrated Circuit).
Electronics & Signal Processing Domain – Low-noise ROIC design, bias control, analog-to-digital conversion, non- uniformity correction (NUC), bad pixel replacement, and image processing electronics for real-time thermal imaging.
Integration & Packaging Domain – Wafer-level vacuum packaging, hermetic sealing, window integration for LWIR transmission, thermal stabilization, detector calibration, and system-level integration into uncooled thermal imaging modules

Application / Use Case

The indigenous microbolometer detector will serve as the primary sensing element in uncooled thermal imaging systems for:
Handheld and weapon-mounted thermal sights
Surveillance and border monitoring systems
Vehicle driver vision enhancement systems
UAV and mast-mounted thermal payloads
Industrial inspection, firefighting, and disaster response equipment.
The detector enables real-time thermal imaging without cryogenic cooling, supporting compact, low-power, rugged systems suitable for field and mobile deployment.

Project Outcome

To establish indigenous capability for the design and manufacture of high-performance microbolometer detector arrays for uncooled thermal imagers, enabling compact, low-power, and maintenance-free thermal imaging systems suitable for large- scale deployment across defense and civilian applications

Expectation from Prototype

Expectation To overcome current capability gaps, prototype development is
Indigenous fabrication of microbolometer FPAs with competitive sensitivity (NETD), uniformity, and pixel operability.
Development of MEMS-based absorber and thermal isolation structures to maximise responsivity.
Integration of a low-noise ROIC for accurate signal readout and on-chip processing support.
Establishment of wafer-level vacuum packaging and hermetic sealing for long-term detector stability.
Demonstration of stable performance across wide ambient temperature ranges without external cooling.
Provision for scalability to higher resolutions and smaller pixel pitches in future upgrades.

Testing / Certification

The developed microbolometer modules will undergo comprehensive performance evaluation and environmental qualification, including:
Thermal sensitivity (NETD) and responsivity measurement
Pixel operability, uniformity, and noise performance testing
Thermal cycling and storage temperature tests
Shock and vibration testing for ruggedisation
Long-term stability and reliability assessment
EMI/EMC compatibility with associated electronics
Certification will be conducted as per relevant military and industrial environmental standards.

Potential Market

Defence Sector: -
Weapon sights and handheld thermal imagers
Surveillance and border monitoring systems
Driver vision systems for armoured and tactical vehicles
UAV and portable ISR payloads
Commercial Sector:-
Applications include industrial inspection, firefighting, building diagnostics, medical screening, automotive night vision, and smart security systems

Business Case

The business case is strong due to the widespread adoption of uncooled thermal imagers across services. Demand drivers include:-
Large-scale induction of portable and vehicle-mounted systems
Replacement and lifecycle support requirements
Upgrade of legacy night vision equipment
Indigenous manufacturing capability can support repeat orders, cost reduction, and export potential to friendly nations and commercial markets.
